## Review

I've read the book thoroughly at first, but then mostly skimmed because of immense frustration.

### What Scythians are we talking about?

I finished the book and still have no idea. He mostly speaks about Royal Scythians of modern Ukraine. You can call them an Empire in a sense that it was a uniform formation. But he also makes claims about Eastern tribes. He glosses over Central Asian tribes. If I were not familiar with the topic, I would be under impression that there was a huge-ass empire spanning the whole region from China to Pontic steppe with one ruler, same religion, same language, etc. But this is objectively false, because this region was divided between different tribes  of _Scythian culture_. Kinda like we talk about Yamnaya culture: there are commonalities, but they are not the same state. 

The biggest revelation for me at some point was that the author wasn't even really talking about Scythians at all. About _any_ of the tribes. Sakas were mentioned only in passing and only in the Appendix he names more or less all Saka tribes. He constantly talks about all the cultures that might have been influenced by Scythians (whoever he means by that word), but he never talks about _them._ He bunched together Saka-Sarmatians, and considered it's enough. Yes, he spends a lot of time describing clothing, arrows, and akinaks, but that's it (for some reason he also omits the third part of Scythian triad: animal style). He didn't spend any time establishing what tribes constitute Scythian culture, how some of them were nomadic, settled, or semi-nomadic (if I recall correctly, he mentioned it in _one_ sentence), how it influenced the differences between them. After reading this book I feel like I need to do a brain cleanse and read some basic Ukrainian and Kazakh schoolbooks to get my knowledge about Scythians from this mush back to be structured. 

From reviews I read from some Iranian academics: (1) great, someone talks about Scythians (2) well.. interesting theories, pity it can't be proved or disproved.

### Book is written badly. 

First, the authors like to repeat the same argument over and over and over again. Even worse, the way he structures his writing, this repetition is presented in way to give a feeling of additional arguments strengthening his claims.

Second, his phrasing:

- It could not have been
- ... are alone sufficient to (..claim..)
- No doubt because...

And, my favorites, these all were pulled from **one** paragraph:
- So there was no question about (..claim..)
- That alone makes it clear...
- Which could only be...
- It's explicit and resoundingly clear...

The more he tried to hammer in his opinion using this language, the less perceptive I became. I don't know, maybe he was right somewhere, but at some point I just tuned out "/s yeah, sure".

Maybe this shouldn't be a book at all? Feels like a big research paper poorly rewritten into a book format. It is also very inconsistent, because somehow appendices and epilogue suddenly rush in a whole bunch of new claims.

(1) Nomadic tribes know what houses are. (2) Not all Scythians were nomadic. (3) Fun bonkers theory is that Zoroaster was born in Arkaim. 


> Both haoma and the barsom are attested among the Scythians. Haoma is attested in the name Sakā Haumavargā in the Naqš-i-Rustam Inscription of Darius the Great (ca. 490 BC), and (though less obviously) in Herodotus’ Greek transcription of the same name, Amyrgioi Sakai. ... and they used haoma for something remarkable enough for it to appear in their national epithet.

On the other hand, the author makes a sweeping claim that "haoma are attested among Scythians" whereas only one particular tribe got named after it. So what is it, is it remarkable or is it common for all Scythians?
